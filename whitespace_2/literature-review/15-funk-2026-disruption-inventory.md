## Background

This is a **literature inventory** of 105 studies on declining
disruptiveness, published 2017–2026 across diverse domains, data
sources, and methodologies. The senior author (Russell Funk) co-
authored the Park-Leahey-Funk 2023 *Nature* paper that is ws2's
positioning target; this paper is the authors' response to the
field's accumulated empirical and methodological literature. The
contribution is positioned as a *resource for researchers and
policymakers* rather than a substantive new finding.

The inventory's core convergence claim:

> "Across measures and domains, that evidence points consistently
> toward decline."

The paper has three substantive uses for ws2:

1. **Reviewer pre-emption.** When a reviewer asks "have you cited
   paper X?" — Funk 2026 likely has X as one of the 105 entries.
   ws2 Methods can cross-reference: *"For an inventory of related
   disruption-decline studies, see Funk et al. 2026."*
2. **Framing the explanation turn.** Funk 2026 explicitly positions
   the field as moving "from documentation to explanation." ws2's
   claim #13 (demographic-vs-intellectual plurality decoupling) is
   on the EXPLANATION side. Introduction language can adopt this
   framing.
3. **Validating ws2's workforce-composition niche claim.** The
   inventory's mechanism survey lists "workforce composition" but
   the actual entries on the topic are about *specialization*,
   *career age*, *generalist decline*, *industry transitions* — NOT
   the demographic-vs-intellectual decoupling specifically. ws2's
   claim #13 is therefore a **specific candidate explanation** that
   Funk 2026 names as under-explored but doesn't itself populate.

This is not a methodologically dense paper — it's a synthesis +
catalog. The close-read value is in (a) the convergence findings,
(b) the exceptions taxonomy, (c) the mechanism survey, (d) the
positioning of specific contested entries (Petersen 2025, Holst
2024, Park 2025), and (e) how ws2 should cite Funk operationally.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The convergence findings (Section 3 Preliminary Synthesis)

Funk 2026 cites four kinds of convergence in the disruption-decline
literature:

**1a. Domain-independent.** "Large-scale studies examining science
and technology as a whole consistently report evidence of decreasing
disruptiveness over time." Examples include scientific papers
(entries 2, 31, 53, 75), patents (entries 96, 95), legal cases
(entry 62), software (entries 96, 95), music (entry 4), visual art
(entry 84 — Japanese woodblock prints, deep-learning Disruption
Index). Field-specific studies in radiology, plastic surgery,
pediatric surgery, colorectal surgery, breast cancer, pharmacy
(entries 20, 9, 8, 7, 19, 10, 103) all show similar patterns.

**1b. Data-source-independent.** "The pattern does not seem to depend
on data source." Cited substrates:
- Crossref (entry 27)
- Microsoft Academic Graph (entries 21, 17, 69, 60, 88, 56, 28, 52,
  82, 24, 79, 78, 46, 81, 31, 16, 33)
- OpenAlex (entries 93, 32, 92, 91, 74, 70, 100, 97, 90)
- SciSciNet (entries 98, 61, 65, 87, 43, 73, 39)
- PubMed (entries 29, 25)
- Dimensions.ai (entries 53, 83)
- Web of Science (entries 51, 13)
- Scopus (entries 51, 13 also use Scopus)
- ACM DL (entry 101)
- USPTO patents (multiple entries)

**1c. Metric-independent.** "The pattern also does not seem to
depend on the specific metric selected." Variants documented:
- CD-index, DI, D, and "numerous variants" (entry 35)
- Product Disruption Index (entries 96, 95)
- Journal Disruption Index (entry 93)
- Decomposed measures of disruption by prior work (entry 98)
- Concept-level disruptiveness based on MeSH (entry 68)
- Global Disruption Index (entry 22)
- Disruptive Knowledge Content index (entry 51)
- Foundation Index (entry 97)

**1d. Method-independent.** Beyond citation-based metrics: text
analysis (entry 3, Bowen et al. 2025 finds disruption fell ~25% by
2010), firm displacement rates (entries 5, 15), product disruption
indices (entries 96, 95), audio features (entry 4 Brazilian Forró),
visual embeddings (entries 55, 84). The non-bibliometric studies
"are unlikely to be vulnerable to limitations of citation-based
measures."

**ws2 implication:** The convergence is strong enough that ws2 doesn't
need to re-litigate "is disruption declining?" — that's settled
empirically. ws2's contribution is a specific **explanation candidate**,
not a re-validation.

### 2. The exceptions / rebounds taxonomy

Funk 2026 (Section 3 + Discussion) identifies "notable exceptions"
which are "potential bright spots, perhaps offering clues into
underlying mechanisms or policy interventions for accelerating
innovation." These are also "methodologically noteworthy — mechanical
artifacts would not be expected to produce structured variation."

Documented exceptions in the inventory:
- **Vaughn et al. 2025** (entry 81): rising disruption in fetal
  surgery 1975–2021. "Fetal surgery tells a different story. Here,
  disruption is not only thriving but also increasing over time."
  Contextual interpretation: a young, evolving field.
- **Tang et al. 2024 iConference** (entry 52): AI research declines
  monotonically 1950–2014, then "since 2014... [it] exhibit[s]
  startling upward trends as 'AI steps into the deep learning
  stage.'" Mechanism candidate: paradigm shift.
- **Macher et al. 2024** (entry 59): rebound in highly disruptive
  patents after 2008, "especially in IT." Mechanism candidate:
  technology cycles.
- **Wu et al. 2025** (entry 24): ML-related economics papers became
  more disruptive after 2010. Mechanism candidate: discipline-import
  effect.
- **Petersen et al. 2025** (entry 86): "stable trends after controls
  in a 1995-2015 sample" — but Funk frames this critically (see
  Key Idea 4 below).

The critical methodological observation:

> "Substantively, these exceptions represent potential bright spots,
> perhaps offering clues into underlying mechanisms or policy
> interventions for accelerating innovation. Methodologically, they
> are also noteworthy — mechanical artifacts would not be expected
> to produce structured variation."

Translation: the existence of exceptions in young/evolving/paradigm-
shift fields *rebuts* the "disruption decline is a measurement
artifact" critique. If the decline were entirely artifactual, it
would appear uniformly across all fields; structured variation
(rebounds in specific subfields) implies a real underlying
phenomenon.

**ws2 implication:** ws2's claim #13 is positioned at the
*explanation* level (what mechanism drives decline), not the
*existence* level. The exceptions taxonomy gives ws2 a defense:
*"if our claim #13 is correct, the decoupling between demographic
and intellectual plurality should be more pronounced in
fields/eras where the decline is steepest, AND less pronounced in
the rebound fields where exceptions exist."* This is testable in
Stage 3.

### 3. The mechanism survey [LOAD-BEARING FOR WS2 NICHE]

Funk 2026 Section 3 closes with a mechanism survey:

> "The literature is increasingly turning from documentation to
> explanation. A number of studies have begun to investigate the
> mechanisms underlying declining disruptiveness, examining factors
> such as team size (entries 2, 69), career age and workforce
> composition (entries 16, 34, 61), the burden of knowledge (entry
> 89), reliance on narrow slices of prior work (entries 30, 75),
> the growing dominance of established ideas and elite researchers
> (entries 104, 11), declining topic switching (entry 79), reduced
> conceptual churn (entry 63), the suppression of paradigm-deviating
> work through peer review (entry 28), the rise of remote
> collaboration (entry 31), the decline of generalist scientists
> (entry 56), and firms consciously preventing disruptive innovation
> (entry 71). While no single mechanism has emerged as definitive,
> the accumulating evidence points toward a constellation of
> interrelated factors."

For ws2, the load-bearing finding is the SPECIFIC content of
"workforce composition" entries:
- **Entry 16 (Coles 2024):** big team science compositional shift
  away from disruption. Big-teams-as-driver, not demographic
  composition.
- **Entry 34:** [need to verify entry text — not in pages I read]
- **Entry 61 (Li et al. 2024 PNAS):** newer cohorts less disruptive
  than older; cohort effect, not demographic effect.
- **Entry 56 (Risha et al. 2024):** "death of Renaissance scientist";
  generalist proportion dropped 55%→40%; "generalist teams 31%
  more likely to produce highly disruptive papers." Specialist-vs-
  generalist composition, not demographic composition.
- **Entry 73 (Zheng & Hou 2025):** elite-driven mode declined 59%→
  10.4%. Elite-vs-non-elite composition, not demographic.
- **Entry 93 (Jurowetzki 2025):** academia-to-industry transitions
  reduce disruptive influence. Industry-vs-academia composition,
  not demographic.

**None of the inventory's "workforce composition" entries test the
demographic-vs-intellectual plurality decoupling.** They test
related but distinct compositional changes (specialization, career
stage, sector). ws2's claim #13 — that demographic plurality has
risen while intellectual plurality has stagnated, AND this
decoupling explains part of the disruption decline — is a SPECIFIC
candidate not yet in the inventory.

This is the **contribution defensibility** for ws2's framing. We
can write in Introduction:

> "Funk et al. 2026 catalog 105 studies establishing that disruption
> decline is robust to data source, metric, and domain. The
> literature is now turning from documentation to explanation,
> examining mechanisms including team size, career age, workforce
> composition (specialization, sector transitions), the burden of
> knowledge, and elite dominance. To date, the demographic
> composition of the scientific workforce has been treated as
> orthogonal to the intellectual diversity of scientific output —
> the assumption being that more demographic diversity yields more
> intellectual diversity. ws2 tests whether this assumption holds
> empirically, by decomposing demographic plurality and intellectual
> plurality into independent annual time series."

### 4. The Petersen 2025 / Park 2025 / Holst 2024 framing (entries 65, 86, 87)

The inventory contains three contested entries that bear directly
on ws2's Tier 1 papers:

**Entry 65 (Holst et al. 2024).** This is our Tier 1 paper #05.
Funk's framing:
> "Using models explicitly designed to correct for alleged biases,
> the authors still find negative and statistically significant
> year effects on disruptiveness for papers (p<0.01) and patents
> (p<0.01) across all specifications (Table S1)."

This is significant — Funk highlights that Holst's *own corrected
analysis* still finds the decline. ws2's own engagement with
Holst (Tier 1 review file) emphasizes Holst's primary critique
(seaborn bug, CD_5=1 dataset artifact). Funk 2026 reminds us that
Holst's Table S1 ALSO contains a corrected analysis that supports
the decline. This is a more nuanced reading of Holst than ws2 has
captured so far.

**Entry 86 (Petersen et al. 2025).** This is our Tier 1 paper #04.
Funk's framing:
> "Argues that after controls, trends are 'at the level of noise.'
> However, the study covers 1995–2015, a period that falls after
> the steepest decline documented in larger-scale studies spanning
> 1945–1990. Additionally, their preprocessing excludes papers with
> fewer than 10 references and more than 1,000 citations —
> restrictions that may bias against finding disruptive work."

Funk is critical of Petersen 2025's methodology: window choice +
preprocessing exclusions. ws2's pre-registration includes Petersen
2025's specification as one of three (per W-W-E review file
discussion); this should be amended to acknowledge Funk's critique.

**Entry 87 (Park et al. 2025).** This is the AUTHORS' response to
Holst. Not yet in our Tier 1 set. Funk's framing:
> "Responds to Holst et al.'s critique that declining disruptiveness
> is an artifact of zero-backward-citation papers. Using critics'
> own dataset, metric, and regression model, finds 'statistically
> and practically significant declines... that equal major benchmark
> transformations in science' (Figs. 1–2, p<0.001). Decline magnitude
> equals the gap between an average paper and a Nobel winner.
> Critics' own regression (their Table S1) shows significant decline
> — 'a finding they neither acknowledge nor interpret.'"

Park 2025 is a sharp response. **Should this be added to ws2's Tier
1?** It's the direct response to a paper we've prioritized.
Probably yes, but as a Tier 1B (positioning read, ~2 hrs) since the
methodology is the same as PLF 2023 which we've already done.

**ws2 implication for Phase 0.2:** Methods's engagement with the
Petersen-Holst-Macher trio should be rebalanced:
- Cite Petersen 2025 as one specification we run (per W-W-E review
  file).
- Acknowledge Funk 2026's critique of Petersen 2025's window +
  preprocessing.
- Cite Holst 2024 for the seaborn-bug + CD_5=1-artifact methodology
  critique.
- ALSO cite Holst 2024 Table S1 as corrected analysis still showing
  decline (Funk-style framing).
- Add Park 2025 response paper as additional Tier 1B reading (or
  cite it briefly in Methods).

### 5. The "documentation to explanation" turn

Funk 2026's central rhetorical move:

> "The literature is increasingly turning from documentation to
> explanation."

This positions the FIELD as having matured past "is disruption
declining?" (settled, yes) into "WHY is it declining?" (open,
contested). ws2 sits squarely in the second phase — testing claim
#13 as one specific explanation candidate.

For ws2 Introduction:

> "Funk et al. 2026 catalog 105 studies establishing that scientific
> and technological disruption is declining across data sources,
> metrics, and domains. The literature is now turning from
> documentation to explanation. ws2 contributes to this explanation
> by testing whether intellectual plurality — the diversity of
> scientific ideas — has decoupled from demographic plurality — the
> diversity of the scientific workforce. If the two have decoupled,
> the conventional theory of cognitive-diversity-as-driver-of-
> innovation requires revision."

This is more confident than the existing Introduction draft and
positions ws2 as participating in a recognized turn rather than
opening a new front.

### 6. Some inventory entries worth flagging beyond the trio

A few specific entries that warrant ws2's attention:

- **Entry 79 (Yang 2025, Inf Process Manag):** "Topic switching is a
  key driver of disruption; a 1SD increase in switching makes papers
  39% more likely to be disruptive. Yet 'real switch scores decline
  over time'." Topic switching is a *behavioral* measure; ws2's
  cluster-occupancy analysis is a *structural* measure. The two are
  related; Yang's mechanism is "scientists who switch topics produce
  more disruption, but switching itself is declining." ws2 Methods
  can engage Yang as a complementary mechanism candidate.

- **Entry 56 (Risha et al. 2024, arXiv):** "death of Renaissance
  scientist" — generalist proportion dropped 55%→40%. This is the
  closest-to-ws2 mechanism in the inventory. Workforce
  *specialization* (in field/topic terms) is related to but
  different from demographic plurality. ws2's claim #13 is more
  specific about demographic-vs-intellectual decoupling.

- **Entry 84 (Shinichi & Matsui 2025, arXiv):** Japanese woodblock
  prints with deep-learning Disruption Index. Non-bibliometric
  rebuttal to "decline is a citation-data artifact" critique. ws2
  can cite this in Methods as an existing precedent for embedding-
  based disruption measurement.

- **Entry 97 (Fang & Evans 2025, arXiv):** "Foundation Index" — work
  that "eclipses prior ideas" — closely aligned with original
  Disruption Index. Fang & Evans (J. A. Evans is the same author as
  Wu-Wang-Evans 2019, our Tier 2A paper #08). Worth tracking as a
  follow-up to W-W-E that may appear in ws2's lifecycle.

- **Entry 75 (Yu et al. 2025, arXiv):** Multiple-substrate replication
  (WoS, OpenAlex, SciSciNet) of disruption decline. Robustness
  showcase. ws2's Stage 3 cross-substrate plan can cite Yu 2025 as
  precedent for the methodology.

- **Entry 95 (Jeong & Lee 2025, Scientometrics):** Product disruption
  on smartphones (12,554 products); finds "products with the
  highest disruption index for all four companies are their initial
  product lines." Product-life-cycle pattern — interesting but
  tangential to ws2.

## Three-Level Results

### Smart-high-schooler reading (~3 min)

This paper is a *catalog*. The authors (a research team that
includes Russell Funk, who co-wrote a famous 2023 *Nature* paper
arguing that scientific disruption has been declining) collected 105
follow-up studies from the past decade. They asked: do these 105
studies, taken together, agree on whether disruption is declining?

The answer is: yes, overwhelmingly. The decline appears in:
- Different kinds of work: scientific papers, patents, software,
  music, visual art, even legal cases.
- Different databases: WoS, Scopus, OpenAlex, PubMed, Dimensions,
  Crossref, USPTO patents.
- Different measurement methods: citation-based metrics, text
  analysis, audio features, visual embeddings, firm-displacement
  economics.

The 5 exceptions documented (e.g., fetal surgery, AI research after
2014) are interesting because *if* the decline were a measurement
artifact, you'd expect uniformity — but the exceptions imply the
phenomenon is real.

For our project: this paper is the citation we use when reviewers
ask "have you considered all related work?" — Funk 2026 has done the
inventory. We engage with specific entries (Petersen 2025, Holst
2024, Park 2025) substantively and reference Funk for the rest.
Importantly, the paper notes that NO study in the 105 has tested
the demographic-vs-intellectual plurality decoupling that we're
testing — so our project is filling a real gap.

co-author of the original Park-Leahey-Funk 2023 *Nature* paper that
ignited the disruption-decline debate — making this paper the
authors' response to the field's accumulated critiques.

Three convergence findings the inventory establishes:

**Domain.** The decline is documented in scientific papers, patents,
products, legal cases, music, visual art. Field-specific case

**Data source.** The decline appears across Crossref, MAG, OpenAlex,
SciSciNet, PubMed, Dimensions, WoS, Scopus, ACM DL — and beyond
bibliometrics, in firm-level Census/Compustat data, audio features,
image embeddings.

**Metric.** Documented across CD-index, DI, D, Product Disruption
Index, Journal Disruption Index, decomposed measures, MeSH-based,
Foundation Index, Disruptive Knowledge Content index.

The paper's central rhetorical move: positioning the field as moving
"from documentation to explanation." The mechanism survey lists
candidate drivers — team size (W-W-E 2019), career age, workforce
composition (specialization vs generalism, sector transitions),
burden of knowledge, elite dominance, declining topic switching,
peer-review suppression, remote collaboration, generalist decline,
firm strategy.

The inventory contains direct positioning-relevant entries for ws2:

### Early-grad reading (~20 min)

Funk 2026 is a *literature inventory* in the strict bibliometric
sense — a structured catalog of 105 studies (2017–2026) bearing on
the question of declining scientific disruptiveness. The catalog
methodology:

> "We identified studies by collecting all citations to four seed
> articles: Funk and Owen-Smith [11], Wu, Wang, and Evans [12], Park,
> Leahey, and Funk [13], and Lin, Frey, and Wu [14]. These papers
> were selected because they developed the core methodology for
> measuring disruptiveness in citation networks and were published
> in high-visibility venues (Nature and Management Science), making
> subsequent empirical work on this topic likely to cite them. Using
> Dimensions.ai, we retrieved 1,587 citing works. We supplemented
> these with manual searches to ensure comprehensiveness, though we
> acknowledge that this citation-based approach may miss relevant
> work that does not cite these particular papers."

Inclusion criteria: peer-reviewed articles, preprints, book chapters,
conference proceedings, theses with original empirical findings on
temporal trends in "disruption" or "disruptiveness." Excluded:
purely theoretical works, pure simulation/formal-model works without
empirical analysis, review articles. Authors note this is a
"conservative lower bound on the total body of evidence."

The "from documentation to explanation" framing has theoretical
content. Documentation is the empirical assertion that disruption is
declining (settled by 100+ converging studies). Explanation is
mechanism — what factors are *causally* driving the decline. The
mechanism survey is the core of the Discussion. The list of
candidates is broad and largely complementary; no single mechanism
"has emerged as definitive."

The inventory's editorial tone is partisan: Funk is co-PI of PLF
2023, and the framing of contested entries (Petersen 2025, Holst
2024) reflects this. ws2 should engage with the inventory critically:
- The convergence findings are likely robust.
- The exceptions taxonomy is methodologically sound.
- The framing of specific contested entries (esp. Petersen 2025)
  is editorially loaded; ws2's pre-registration treats Petersen
  2025 as a valid alternative specification, not a flawed analysis
  to be dismissed.

For ws2 Methods + Introduction, the main inheritance is the
**workforce-composition niche claim defensibility**. The inventory
lists "workforce composition" as a mechanism but the actual entries
(56, 16, 34, 61, 73, 93) are about specialization, big-team shift,
generalist decline, sector transition — NOT demographic-vs-
intellectual decoupling. ws2 fills a specific gap.

The paper's reviewer-pre-emption value is significant: when reviewers
ask "have you cited paper X?" — odds are X is in Funk's inventory.
ws2 can cite Funk 2026 in Methods for the broad literature engagement,
and engage individual entries only where they're methodologically
load-bearing.

### CF1 — Editorial partisanship — should ws2 take Funk at face value?

Funk is co-PI of PLF 2023 (the headline disruption-decline paper).
The inventory's framing of Petersen 2025 (critical) and Holst 2024
(emphasizing the corrected-analysis decline) reflects this. ws2's
pre-registration treats Petersen 2025 as a valid alternative
specification. **Should ws2's Methods cite Funk 2026's framing
neutrally, or take Funk's critique of Petersen 2025 as our position
too?**

Recommendation: cite Funk's framing neutrally; report both
specifications per pre-registration; let the reader weigh.

### CF2 — Adding Park 2025 to Tier 1?

Park 2025 (entry 87) is the authors' direct response to Holst (our
Tier 1 #05). On methodology grounds it's redundant with PLF 2023
(same authors, same metric, same datasets), but on rhetoric
it's the closing volley in the methodology debate. **Does ws2 need
to engage Park 2025 substantively?**

Recommendation: add as Tier 1B (~2 hrs positioning read), cite
briefly in Methods + Discussion. Not load-bearing for Phase 0.2
pre-registration.

### CF3 — Reading Holst Table S1 differently

Funk's framing of Holst entry 65 emphasizes the corrected-analysis
decline. ws2's existing Tier 1 review of Holst (review file 05)
emphasizes the seaborn-bug + CD_5=1 artifact. **Are these two
readings compatible, or does Funk's framing weaken the Holst
critique we've absorbed?**

Recommendation: both readings are compatible. Holst's critique
identifies a specific artifact that affects the magnitude of the
decline; Holst's corrected analysis still finds decline. ws2 cites
both readings — the artifact critique justifies our non-use of
CD-index, AND the corrected-analysis-still-finds-decline supports
the broader phenomenon framing.

### CF4 — Which "workforce composition" entry is closest to ws2?

Funk's mechanism survey lumps "workforce composition" under several
entries (16, 34, 56, 61, 73, 93). The closest to ws2's claim #13 is
**entry 56 (Risha 2024 — generalist scientist decline)** — workforce
*specialization* maps loosely onto ws2's intellectual-vs-demographic
plurality decoupling, in the sense that high specialization means
"each person engages narrower topics." But the demographic axis
(gender × country × prestige) is orthogonal to specialization.
**Should ws2 explicitly engage Risha 2024 in Discussion, or treat
it as parallel/complementary?**

Recommendation: Discussion engages Risha 2024 as a parallel
mechanism; ws2 frames the demographic-vs-intellectual decoupling
as a third independent axis (not specialization, not career age,
but demographic-intellectual joint composition).

### CF5 — Citation depth for Funk 2026

Funk is a recent preprint (Feb 2026); not yet peer-reviewed at time
of writing. **Should ws2 cite Funk as preprint with caveats?**

Recommendation: cite as preprint at draft stage; track its
